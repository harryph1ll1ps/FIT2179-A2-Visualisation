Nuclear: The World's Most Controversial Energy Source

Feared by many, misunderstood by most — nuclear power generates more electricity with fewer deaths than almost any other energy source. So why does the world remain so divided?

Created by: Harry Phillips  |  Date: 26/05/2026

Introduction

Nuclear energy sits at the centre of one of the defining debates of our time. On one side: a dense, reliable, low-carbon power source capable of running at over 90% capacity day and night. On the other: the spectre of Chernobyl, Fukushima, and a waste problem with no clean end.

This visualisation cuts through the fear and the hype to look at what the data actually says — about how deadly nuclear really is, how much power it produces, what it costs to fuel, and how it compares to the alternatives. Australia, notably, operates zero nuclear plants. Is that the right call?

How the world's electricity mix has shifted, 2000–2025

Each bar shows the share of global electricity from fossil fuels, nuclear, and renewables. Over 25 years renewables have nearly doubled — from 19% to 34% — while nuclear has quietly slipped from 17% to under 9%. Fossil fuels still supply the majority.

Source: Our World in Data / Ember (2026) — Share of electricity generation

How different countries generate their electricity

Each row is a country and each column an energy source — the darker the cell, the larger the share. The contrast is stark: Australia still draws roughly two-thirds of its power from fossil fuels and nothing from nuclear, while France and Sweden run almost entirely on low-carbon nuclear and renewables.

Source: Our World in Data — Electricity mix (2025)

Where are the power plants?

Every grid-scale generating station on Earth, coloured by fuel. Fossil-fuel plants blanket the map, while the relatively few pink nuclear stations cluster in North America, Europe and East Asia.

Source: global-power-plant-database.csv — World Resources Institute (WRI)

Global nuclear generation, month by month

The world's reactors put out a fairly steady ~220 TWh of electricity every month. But nuclear's share of global generation has slipped — from about 11% in 2019 to roughly 9% — as total demand and renewables grow faster than the nuclear fleet.

Source: world-electricity-generation.csv — Nuclear Energy Institute (NEI)

The price of uranium — boom, bust, and renaissance

Uranium fuel sat near US$10 per pound through the early 2000s, spiked as demand surged, then slumped after the 2011 Fukushima accident chilled the industry. Prices have climbed sharply again in the 2020s as nuclear returns to favour.

Source: uranium-purchase-price-us.csv — US Energy Information Administration (EIA)

Safe and clean — or neither?

Plotting deaths against emissions per unit of electricity, nuclear lands in the safest, cleanest corner — alongside wind and solar, and far below coal, oil and gas on both measures. Bubble size shows each source's share of world electricity.

Source: death-rates-by-energy-source.csv — Our World in Data

One reactor. A thousand turbines worth of power.

Energy density in perspective: a single nuclear reactor matches the annual output of hundreds of wind turbines.

Source: Our World in Data (ourworldindata.org/nuclear-energy)

Every major nuclear incident since 1952

In seven decades of nuclear power, only two events have ever reached Level 7 — the top of the international severity scale: Chernobyl in 1986 and Fukushima in 2011. Every other rated incident sits well below them.

Source: nuclear-incidents.csv — International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA)

Some countries are leaning into nuclear, others wait

Nuclear's share of national electricity, country by country. A handful of nations — led by France — lean heavily on it (deep pink), while most of the world generates little or none.

Source: share-electricity-nuclear.csv — Our World in Data

France exports far more than it imports

France generates far more electricity than it consumes and sells the surplus to its neighbours — a direct dividend of the world's most nuclear-heavy grid. It exports several times more than it imports.

Source: RTE — Commercial electricity trading between France and neighbouring countries (2025)

Conclusion

The data makes a compelling case that nuclear energy's public image is far more dangerous than the technology itself. Its safety record, reliability, and low-carbon output make it a serious candidate for any nation's energy future — including Australia's.
